Context

Yesterday, I saw a series of tweets tweets by Andy Mangold that caught my attention.

I’m going to group them together to make them more readable:

August stats for reddit: PageViews: 3.4 billion, Uniques: 42.9 million, Av. Time Spent per Visit: 17:29

— reddit (@reddit) September 5, 2012

Do the math on those numbers I just retweeted from @reddit. Humans spent a combined 1431 years on Reddit in August.

— Andy Mangold (@andymangold) September 5, 2012

This is staggering to me. What did they accomplish in those 1431 years? We are living in the age of distraction.

— Andy Mangold (@andymangold) September 5, 2012

Then, he shared a link to a blog post by Daniel Benneworth-Gray, which I’ll also quote:

Dr Luis von Ahn of Carnegie Mellon University estimated that in 2003, nine billion human hours were spent playing computer solitaire. To put this in context, the construction of the Panama Canal took 20 million human-hours.

So there.

I’d like to touch on this argument — which I see made from time to time — because I think it’s total crap. To be clear: this has nothing to do with Andy or Daniel. They both fine gentlemen who just happen to have triggered the thought in my mind.

Now, having said that — it’s absolutely ridiculous to try to measure the time value of interaction with Reddit by aggregating the total years spent on the site in a month, and even more ridiculous to compare that artificial measure to the time spent on manual labor / construction.

What these “statistics” remind me of are those “news” sites that report a “poll of polls.” They act as if you can simply take the mean of a series of polls and derive an accurate and valuable projection from it. You can’t. It’s a load of crap. And the editors know it.

To cite the number of aggregate years spent on an activity and present it as outrages is meaningless without context. It’s akin to how politicians like to tell you how the government wastes $## billion on whatever program they don’t like. Sure, it’s a lot of money. But as a proportion of the overall budget, it’s practically nothing. Context. It matters.

And I feel like if you’re going to cite those numbers to make a point, you have some obligation to put them into context — particularly because they’re such abnormal measures. For example, if people spent 9 billion hours playing solitaire in 2003, how many hours were spent driving? Taking out the trash? Showering? Building products for sale? Etc.? Etc.? Put the number into perspective; maybe 9 billion hours isn’t that much, considering that there are 7 billion people on Earth.

We spend a lot of time talking about productivity, focus, and time management in the online circles I frequent — and for good reason. But it’s important that, when we do so, we really are able to understand what we’re talking about. And without context, citing the aggregate number of years is just meaningless — a big number meant for the shock value.
