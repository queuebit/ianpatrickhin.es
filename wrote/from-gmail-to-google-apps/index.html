From Gmail to Google Apps

When Gmail first hit the scene back in 2004, I was among its earliest adopters. A full time volunteer for Howard Dean at the time, I had known a number of folks who had initial beta invites and managed to snag one. So it was that ianhines {@} gmail.com became my first “permanent” email address. 

To be entirely truthful, I can’t even remember any of the addresses I had prior to my first Gmail because they were that un-memorable. Gmail’s launch was timed perfectly for me: I was 18-years old, about to graduate high school, and just beginning to have a real interest in projecting a mature, professional face to the world. Gone were the days of ‘LacrosseGuy1234’ type usernames. I was going to college, and I wanted my email address to reflect it.

The Gmail solution served me well for years. I currently have more than 30,000 emails archived, and have yet to reach 20% of my account’s memory limit. Gmail’s Spam-Blocking, Search, and Conversation Threading features make storing and retrieving emails nearly effortless. In fact, Google is so good at storing and retrieving messages that during my college years I took to using my inbox as a flash drive, storing my important documents in my inbox instead of on my hard drive.

It is, quite nearly, the perfect email solution. Nearly.

The Permanency Concern

Facebook’s recent Terms of Service scandal was a real wake up call for me. I suddenly realized that the web-services I used on a daily basis could up and change at a moment’s notice.

What if Gmail suddenly asserted proprietary control over the contents of all of my emails? I would cancel my service, that’s what. But then I would lose control over the only email address I use. Suddenly hundreds of friends, family, and professional contacts wouldn’t know how to reach me; tens of thousands of archived emails would be lost.

This potential chain of events was most definitely unacceptable. There had to be a way to preserve the Gmail service I know and love without relying on an email address that I, ultimately, did not control. Then it hit me: I needed to host my own email. Sure, it wouldn’t be free (like Gmail had been for years), but I would have the security of knowing that the address was permanent.

ian {@} ianhines.com & Google Apps

I began hosting my own email about two weeks ago here on ianhines.com, and I immediately remembered why I have long hated traditional Web Mail. The interface was awkward, the folder structure rigid, and worst of all—no search integration. After nearly five years of Gmail there was no turning back, so like any good Gmail loyalist I installed Google Apps on my server.

The installation went smoothly (though not quickly, but that’s another entry all together), and pretty soon ian {at} ianhines.com was up and running with full Gmail functionality. Now I had the best of both worlds: a static, proprietary email address and the Gmail interface. I immediately went to work ensuring that all my various Gmail accounts forwarded to my new address (I have more than one), as I don’t have to tell you what an annoyance it can be to have to check multiple email accounts (even with MailPlane.app). I must say I was quite pleased with myself, and went to bed that night quite satisfied.

But, as I’m sure many of you were already thinking, it was too good to be true. The next morning it hit me: forwarding or not, all of my 30,000+ emails are stored on an account that I won’t be logging into anymore, undercutting the very purpose of having the Gmail interface. Gmail’s has a built in solution to this problem, which essentially ‘links’ two account via POP3 and thereby downloads your messages to the new server, but this solution wouldn’t work for me. There were simply too many messages (30,000+, or ~ 1 GB) spread over too many Labels for it to work effectively or in any reasonable timeframe.

I needed another solution, and what I found was YippieMove.
