My Priest’s Name

The priest at my local parish is a really great guy. He’s soft spoken, affable, funny and has a memorable mustache. Last weekend, he sought out my wife — who is pregnant with our third child — to bless her and the baby, after Mass.

But here’s the thing: I can’t remember his name.

Mind you, I’ve only been a parishioner at my parish since December, when I moved into my current home Los Angeles County. But I’ve been to Mass every Sunday, and you’d think I’d have learned it by now.

Yet, when I think about it, I didn’t know the name of the priest at my last parish, either. In fact, I have to go all the way back to RCIA before I can think of a priest who I knew well enough to know by name.

The trouble is that (most) parish priests aren’t meeting their flock where they are: online.

I work 10 hours per day, commute an hour each way and have to raise two small children. I don’t have time to attend faith formation groups that meet at 7:30pm. By the time Mass has ended, my children are so restless that I have to whisk them out the side door, lest they cause a scene. In that reality, the priest becomes a functionary — not a friend — which can only hurt the Church.

But this doesn’t have to be. With Twitter, Facebook, email and sites like this one, parish priests and deacons can build real, meaningful relationships with their parishioners online. Then, they can reinforce those relationships in person on Sunday.

What I’m talking about is Digital Organizing: the process of turning online engagement (ex: Twitter conversations) into offline action (ex: increased Mass attendance). In the past few years it’s transformed how politicians and nonprofits engage their supporters, and it can breathe new life into parishes the world over, too.
