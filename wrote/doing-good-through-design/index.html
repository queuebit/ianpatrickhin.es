Doing good through design

I’m would not describe myself as a designer, but for years now I’ve had a passion for good design. And not just web design, either. Program design. Systems design. Form design. Industrial design. Design. Design. Design.

I’ve been thinking a lot lately about what it is about design that draws me to it, and I think I’ve got it. Or, more accurately, I’ve rediscovered it in a 2011 interview I did with Stephen Hackett of 512 Pixels. In it, I said:

I think that good design makes the world a better place, and that Apple’s product line is one of the prime examples of good design in the modern marketplace.

The reason I feel that way about good design is because good design is the result of a continuous process of reexamination and revision, with an eye towards making the best possible product. That’s really what life’s about, no matter what you do: an endless process of reevaluation and revision, with a goal on doing the very best job possible.

Good design makes the world a better place. Or, put another way, you can do good through design. In fact, that may well be what makes good design good: it makes the world a better place.

I didn’t realize it at first, but that’s why I came to work at NationBuilder: to help design and ship a product that empowers people to to connect with one another and make the world a better place. For me, that ethos was like a source of gravity that I couldn’t avoid.

We’re empowering activists, social entrepreneurs, faith organizations and leaders in developing nations to bring change to their communities — to turn their lives into a movement. And every time I see a customer use our product to catalyze a change in their community my eyes light up.

We’re doing good through design, and that’s what it’s all about.
