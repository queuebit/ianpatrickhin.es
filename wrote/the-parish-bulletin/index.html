The Parish Bulletin

I can say with confidence that your parish is publishing a weekly ‘bulletin.’ It contains the names and contact information of clergy and parish administrators, a weekly message from the priest, a schedule of events and a bucket load of advertisements. But does anyone read it? How do you know?

Oh, sure, people take it. It’s practically shoved into their hands as they leave mass. But do they read it. The truth is that you have no idea — and neither does your priest.

Nearly everything that’s published in the weekly bulletin could, and should, be published online. Exclusively online, where it’s always available and its use can be measured. It would be far cheaper to publish a single, responsive and dynamic website than to print and distribute thousands of paper bulletins each week. And it would also be more accessible and useful for parishioners. And no, uploading a PDF of your print bulletin doesn’t count.

So why don’t more parishes go web-only? Two reasons: know-how and economics.

The truth is that most priests — and most parish staff — still don’t know how to effectively use the Internet to organize their parish community. The don’t know where to start — or, worse, might think they’re already doing it well — so they just keep doing what they’re doing. That’s a tough problem to solve, but its solvable.

The more vexing problem is economic. Parishes earn money from those annoying bulletins. Yep — the advertising. Many parishes cannot afford to phase out their antiquated and low-performing bulletins because they’re reliant on the advertising pages to keep their financial heads above water. This creates a perverse incentive: what’s best for the parish’s pocketbook is not what’s best for its evangelical mission.

In the 21st-century, Catholic parishes must pivot their engagement strategies to meet people where they are: online. I am literally typing this blog post on a mobile computer I carry in my pocket. Most people carry a computer in their pocket. They search the web on the go. They do not keep their bulletins long enough to reference them on Sunday afternoon, much less later that week.

So in the coming week, I am going to tear apart my own parish bulletin to show how it can be better done online. I’ll publish a new post here outlining how a different aspect of the bulletin could be reflected on a website like this one. And maybe, just maybe, one of you will take one of those ideas back to your parish community and give it a go.
