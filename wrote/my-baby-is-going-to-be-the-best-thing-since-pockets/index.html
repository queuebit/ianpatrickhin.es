My baby is going to be the best thing since pockets.

My whole life I’ve always heard about how a parent’s love for their child is unlike anything I’ve ever known. You know the stories: “No matter what I’m always proud of you,” or, “When I had my children nothing else seemed as important anymore.” I always had a little trouble understanding that until recently when, just as had always been predicted, I started to get it when I had my own kids (or in my case, will have).

The first few weeks after I find out I was having a baby were spent just trying to get my mind around the concept. It’s a pretty radical change, and even though I was thrilled it still took a lot of getting used to. The first moment I heard about it was filled with the most indescribable combination of excitement, fear, pride, and worry I’ve ever had. But once it started to grow, and Christy started showing, I started (it’s still happening) to feel like the father everyone always told me I would.

It really is exactly like you’ve heard: that once you’ve got a child nothing else seems as important. But what’s more surprising is that that feeling is almost a relief. It actually feels pretty good, after years of college & career planning, to just focus on what’s best for my family—to have a singular focus around which to plan everything else.

Christy has always been very supportive of me doing whatever I wanted in terms of my career, and as a result I’ve always been very intent on making sure I do work that I enjoy. But over the last few weeks it’s become increasingly clear to me that the line of work I had been focusing on me would, by its very nature, take me aware from my family and make me (in my view) a poor father. That is unacceptable to me, and though I’m not sure what changes I’ll make I know that the plan as previously constituted no longer suits the circumstances.

And that’s not a problem, because my kid’s going to be the best thing since pockets, and that’s just on Day One. I want to be home for dinner, read bedtime stories, and take walks in the park. I want to be there for sports practice, parent-teacher conferences, and weekend day trips. Most of all I want a job I can count on, so that I always know when I need to be at work and when I’ll be at home; I want to do everything I can do be sure that I don’t ever have to worry about where my next paycheck is going to come from, or whether I can afford a doctor’s visit.

Nothing else seems as important as those things anymore, because someone’s going to call me Dad. And “Dad” means “Make-sure-everything-works-out-okay Guy”.
