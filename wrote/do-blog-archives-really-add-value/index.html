Do blog archives really add value?

When I started building this current version of my website, I spent a lot of time thinking about how to display individual posts, the homepage and colophon. But honestly? The last thing on my mind was the archives.

That’s not to say I didn’t care about archiving. I’ve been working every night for a week to migrate over each of the 80+ articles I’d written since 2009. I reformatted their HTML to make sure they properly displayed in NationBuilder, revised the site’s overall tagging system and updated their metadata to ensure good SEO. But that’s all to do with individual posts. In terms of a ‘Table of Contents’ style archive, well, this site has none.

I don’t have any reliable data, but my sense is that archives pages — in general — are going the way of the buffalo. I spent some time yesterday looking through some of the most popular “blogs” on the web (according to Technorati and The Guardian), and ya know what? Most of them don’t bother with archives in a meaningful way. The exceptions are all part of the old guard: Kottke, Gruber, Dooce, etc.

I have some theories about why this might be, but I think the core of it likely comes back to two facts about the web today:

  1. Twitter has made the web more ephemeral; and
  2. Google search results are so good that it’s the defactor archive for everything.

The web is a very much a “now” medium. How often have you gone through your RSS reader and bulk archived everything written any more than a few days ago? A lot, right? People are mostly discovering their new articles via social media, and if it’s two days old, it’s stale. Newer publishing platforms like Roon and Tumblr essentially don’t bother with archives, preferring pagination. This undermines the value of traditional archives in (what I believe to be) obvious ways.

But search is an even bigger factor. When I want to look up a review that I remember reading on Shawn Blanc’s site, I don’t dig through his archives (which are fantastic… by the way). I google it. And what’s more, googling it is super efficient. The only time I browse someone’s archives is when I’m just plain curious, and then they’re only useful when they’re tag or category driven.

I should say that this realization came as a bit of a surprise to me. I come from a generation and community of bloggers that put a lot of time into curating and designing our archives. But in retrospect, I wonder whether that was an intentional, data driven decision or whether we simply built them because Movable Type and WordPress told us we should. Knowing what I know now, I suspect the latter.
