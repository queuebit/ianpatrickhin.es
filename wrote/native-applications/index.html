Native applications

Yesterday, I decided that — wherever possible — I would start trying to use the native applications for my software of choice.

This is hardly an uncontroversial decision. Many of the most beautifully designed applications for the Mac and iOS are third-party apps for popular services, like Twitter or Gmail. Apps like Mail.app, Mailbox or Tweetbot have long defined my experience the web — and for the better. So, you may be asking yourself, why leave them behind?

The answer is: for the designers. No, not the independent, third-party designers — though they certainly deserve our unending respect and appreciation. I mean for the product designers at the services I rely on, like Twitter’s design team or Google’s Gmail team. They work hard craft the ideal user experience for their services, and I’ve recently become very interested in experiencing their product as it was designed and helping them to continually improve.

Part of this stems from working at NationBuilder, where I’m very involved with our own product team. We put a lot of thought into designing the ideal customer experience for our software, and it’s really made me appreciate how much attention to detail is put into the official clients for services like Gmail and Twitter in a way that I didn’t before. Moreover, I’ve become increasingly aware of how a healthy feedback loop of bug reports and feature suggestions helps to make a product better over time.

So as an act of thanksgiving for the services I love so much — and the people who lovingly craft them — I’m going to dedicate myself to first-party services. I’m going to report bugs, suggest features and generally contribute to the feedback loop that I’m working to encourage within my own team. Because I can’t expect that sort of buy in from others if I’m not willing to do it myself.
