Facebook Paper

Today, Facebook announced Paper — a new iOS app that reimagines how its users experience Facebook on their mobile devices. And, as one would expect, it’s been met with heaps of praise from the tech blog industry and a rash of criticism from designers and developers on Twitter. I think that it’s fantastic, personally, but that’s not the point of this post.

Most of the criticism that I’ve seen about the app — which becomes available on February 3rd — focuses on the fact that most of the content being shared in the promo video is gorgeous, whereas most of the content your Facebook friends share is, well, not. So what? Facebook has designed a beautiful picture frame; are they to blame if their users fill it with ugly photographs? No. They are not.

And moreover, it’s important to remember that the experience of Facebook is different for each of its users. I believe that most everyday Facebook users genuinely enjoy the content they see on the platform. It’s why they keep coming back. They like seeing updates about their nieces and nephews playing at the playground, or about the struggles of parenting young kids, or whatever. That’s what Facebook is, for them: a place to stay connected with family and old friends they rarely have occasion to see.

I think Paper is going to be a hit for Facebook. But even if it isn’t, faulting them for making a beautiful frame for their users’ content — whatever that may be — seems narrow-minded and devalues the work they’ve put into making it.
