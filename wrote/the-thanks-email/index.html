The “Thanks Email”

Gabe Weatherhead over at MacDrifter has chimed into the ongoing discussion (I feel like it resurfaces every few months) about whether or not sending a one-word email saying “Thanks” is polite or rude.

The folks on the “polite” side tend to say that it’s just good manners. The folks on the “rude” side say that unnecessary email wastes everyone’s time. I’m on both sides of the fence.

To offer some real-world perspective, I’ve tried not sending “thanks emails.” It didn’t go well.

In a white collar office job, email etiquette is a fair bit different from what you might think if you spent all your time reading GTD blogs and listening to podcasts about email. In that world, not sending a “thank you” email will quickly earn you a reputation as someone who’s a pain to work with.

Saying “thank you” tells the other person that you appreciate their time — especially when you’ve asked them for something and they’ve delivered. If you don’t say “thank you,” it won’t take long for that person to develop a perception of you as a demanding, bossy, rude person.

This is especially true if you predominantly interact with this person through email. Email is tone-neutral, which most often (in my experience) means that the recipient will imagine a more harsh tone than you intended. That’ll quickly eat away at your chits, and give you a reputation that you don’t want. Trust me. I know.

So feel it out. If you’re emailing Dave Caolo, don’t say “thanks.” If you’re emailing a colleague at the office, maybe consider doing so.

“In this, as in all things, context is king.”
