Create frameworks, not solutions

Growing up in Maryland, one of the things I dreaded the most was driving in downtown Washington, DC. Anyone who’s ever driven in it knows that The District has an incredibly complex street grid that just makes driving without turn-by-turn navigation absolutely unbearable. There’s a diagonal grid overlaid on top of the normal street grid, with traffic circles thrown about just for good measure, making it incredibly easy to find yourself totally disoriented.

One place, in particular, that still leaves me with stressed where I regularly had this problem was Dupont Circle. The circle has not less than nineteen entries and exits, and is in fact two concentric circles. As a perpetual newbie, I always felt that it was sheer madness. How were there not constant crashes in this mess of an intersection?! And yet, there weren’t. It worked.

The reason it worked isn’t because Dupont Circle is so incredibly well designed. The success of Dupont Circle is a direct result of the genius of the framework on which it rests: the rules of the road. We take them for granted, but at some point someone had to design the laws that govern traffic. They didn’t design all the world’s roads: they created a coherent, scalable system for managing unpredictable scenarios in a way that keeps everyone safe, alive and en route. And in so doing, they changed the world.

That’s something that’s true of all of my favorite software products: they’re really creating a system — a framework — for their users. They don’t worry so much about how people are using it, or even if they’re using it “right,” so long as its useful to them. And by designing a good system it empowers their users to create value in the lives of the people around them.
