Leashing Your Children

From time to time, I see a parent dragging their children through a public space by a leash. And every time I do, it makes my stomach turn.

As a father myself, I can (almost) understand the thought process that would lead someone to leash their kids. I’m sure that the rationalization starts on a practical level — “It will be too crowded for the stroller…” — and seems totally reasonable to them. But it’s an awful idea.

When you put your children on a leash you dehumanize them. You’ve subconsciously given yourself permission to ignore them. When they fail to keep pace — or stray too far — you’ll yank on the leash. They’ve essentially become baggage you’re dragging along.

Also, consider the psychological impact of being leashed on the children. They’re either (a) being dragged behind a parent who is ignoring them or (b) being left to run rampant by a parent too indifferent to pay attention to them. They’re definitely not receiving the signal that they’re important to their parents.

To be clear: managing children in a public place is hard. It requires constant attention, patience and love. But it is imperative that parents give it their all.

Set high expectations for behavior in public. Make your children walk if your hands are full. But make them walk with you: not ahead of you, not behind you and not wherever they please. You’re teaching them the discipline and self-control that they’ll need to be a successful adult.

The bottom line is this: a leash is for something you own, not for someone you love. Please, don’t put it on your kids.
