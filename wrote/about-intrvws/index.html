About Intrvws

I think it was about this time last year that I originally had the idea to interview people. I had come to realize that I was spending a significant portion of my day, every day, reading tweets and blog posts by virtual strangers. I knew an awful lot about what they thought about their obsessions, but very little about them. I didn’t know them as people, only editorial voices.

That would not do.

I remember quite distinctly that someone had told me that one of the best ways to learn something is to ask leading questions and listen. I had read a number of interviews on other blogs—most notably by Shawn Blanc—and thought “what a neat way to get to know everyone.” So I started asking people: “would you be interested in doing an interview for my blog?” Not a single person said no.

But, as so often is the case with best laid plans, life happened. I lost my job, and began taking interviews for new positions. A lot of interviews. And a question kept creeping up in them: “can you tell me a little bit about your blog?”

See, I work in a business where having a blog can be a particularly dicey affair. For that reason I try to keep a very stark separation between my personal affairs and my professional ones but, not knowing me, potential employers understandably felt that my blog presented too great a risk to them. After watching a few promising positions slip through my fingers, I decided it was time: the blog had to go.

I was truly blessed that I was only out of work for about a month, which in this economy is certainly more a cause for thanksgiving. With the paralyzing fear of longterm unemployment behind me (and trust me, it is a paralyzing fear), I gradually resumed my hobby but with a much diminished readership and no real sense of direction.

From Interviews to Intrvws

When I withdrew my blog, I offered each of the interviewees the opportunity to publish their interviews—which were in various stages of completion—to their respective websites so that they would not have been done in vain. They did, and much to my surprise the response was resoundingly enthusiastic. It seems that I was not the only person eager to learn more about the people behind the writing.

But Intrvws, as a dedicated project, didn’t begin to take shape until several months later. It wasn’t until Patrick Rhone, whose early encouragement cannot be overstated, convinced me that this was a project worth pursuing that I decided it deserved a home of its own. He couldn’t have been more right.

I’ve struggled for rather a long time with a desire to create something that touched people. I’ve never been a particularly gifted artist, musician, or writer—and I know that. I can’t really build things, either—digital or otherwise.

I’m a verbal person. A professional talker. But as a web enthusiast, I’ve always struggled to find a way to translate that passion for conversation and discussion into to something special to share with others in a medium so dominated by print and images.

But now I think I may have finally found a way.

When I set out to conduct an interview, my goal is to steer a conversation that brings out the best in the interviewee—and the reader. It’s really not about me at all. And maybe that’s what makes it so fun.

I want Intrvws to be the sort of website where the readers await the next installment with anticipation. I’m not keeping it on a strict schedule—it’s far more important that the interviews have the time to develop naturally than that I post by some artificial deadline. I also don’t start the interviews with a pre-planned set of questions. It’s not really a Q&A at all—it’s a conversation meant for sharing.

I don’t really know where Intrvws is going. I don’t know how many I’ll do, or how often. I don’t really have a plan at all. All I know is that I want to create the sort of content that readers decline to read when it’s published in favor of sending it to Instapaper to read with their Sunday morning coffee.
