44 GB

By most people’s standards, I use a very old, and very weak, MacBook. Specifically, I use an early 2009 white MacBook—the old plastic ones no one remembers anymore. It’s not a “Pro,” and it doesn’t perform like it. But at $999, it was all I could afford. And I was through with Windows.

Despite its hardware shortcomings, my MacBook has served me well in the 18-months I’ve had it. It has been well equipped to handle everything I’ve thrown at it—albeit not all at the same time—and now comprises the anchor in my suite of Mac devices (alongside my iPhone 3GS and iPad, both 16GB models). Together these devices make quite a formidable team, and it’s a rare occasion when I find myself wanting more.

But I’d be lying if I didn’t say that the addition of the iPad has made me realize my aging MacBook’s biggest liability: speed. This is a slow machine. And frankly, that’s a bit sad to say, considering it’s only 18-months old. But it’s true, all the same. Try to do too many things, and it grinds to a screeching halt—with nothing but the pinwheel of doom to console me.

I used to think that this was a result of insufficient RAM (it only has 2GB), but I’m increasingly convinced that’s only part of the problem. Again, using my iPad has taught me that you can do incredible things with relatively weak hardware (processor and RAM) by having a Solid State Drive (SSD) as your startup disc. And so, with the many Amazon Gift Cards I received this Christmas I bought myself an Intel X25-M 80GB SSD.

I’ve heard nothing but good things about SSDs, aside from complaints about the price. Ben Brooks, Patrick Rhone, John Gruber, John Rust—they all swear by them. So I took their advice, and decided to make the switch. The SSD shipped yesterday, and should be waiting for me when I return from Texas on Sunday.

But when I tell people about my purchase, they’re usually surprised. “Only 80GB,” they say. “Aren’t you worried that’s not enough?” Well, no. I’m not.

My MacBook currently has a 120GB HDD, of which I am using a whopping 44.25GB. Yes, you read that right, 44.25GB. That’s all of my apps, documents, and system files. Plus the OS. 44.25GB, and holding relatively stable.

How is it that I’m able to get by with so little internal storage? I avoid unnecessary clutter like the plague.

Having a 15-month old, my house is rarely, if ever, tidy for more than a few hours at a time. And that’s as it should be. But for peace of mind, I need order—a place for everything, and everything in its place—and it’s on my MacBook that I find it. I keep my drives clean and orderly, my desktop empty, and my windows neatly sorted into 9 distinct Spaces. It’s like digital feng shui.

Practically speaking, I suppose there’s really four real tricks to keeping my internal storage needs low:

  1. No Photoshop: I am not a graphic designer by any means, so I don’t have all the related digital collateral that comes with that field. Those files are large, and so for those of you who work with them regularly the need for a large drive is necessarily more urgent.
  2. Avoid unnecessary applications: If I don’t need it, I don’t keep it. Or, better, I don’t download it in the first place.
  3. Save, at most, 1-2 versions of anything: For most things I am working on I end up with two files—a “draft” and a “final.” I tend not to save multiple drafts. It’s just not necessary.
  4. Keep media on an external hard drive: I have a 500GB Western Digital Passport drive that houses my iTunes and Aperture Libraries. You might say this is cheating—that it means I’m not really using only 44GB—but these are simply files I don’t need on an all-the-time basis that require massive storage space. Moving them off the internal drive was the best computing decision I’ve ever made. I highly recommend it.

I made each of these decisions for independent reasons rather than as part of some coherent strategy for shrinking my HD footprint. I genuinely didn’t realize how little I was using until I started shopping for an SSD. I mention this because it goes to show you that you might be surprised to find out how little you actually need.

But, now that I’ve started thinking about it, I think I can use even less. I’m hoping that on my new SSD I’ll use less than 40GB, and perhaps even approach 35GB. How? Three steps, the first two of which will be entirely painless.

  1. Exclusively use the free 2GB Dropbox account for file storage: I already keep all of my documents on a free Dropbox account, and I am only using 1.12GB. That 1.12GB includes every file I’ve created or saved over the last 18 months, including all of my personal files from my last three jobs.

  2. Only install 15 third-party applications:
    • Dropbox: For the reasons mentioned above.
    • OmniFocus: My GTD application of choice.
    • Yojimbo: My everything bucket.
    • iWork: Pages, Number, and especially Keynote are life-savers.
    • Tweetie: Its feature set is outdated, but it’s home nonetheless.
    • Reeder (beta): Even in beta, it’s still the best feed reader I’ve ever used.
    • Coda: For my HTML, CSS, and routine FTP needs.
    • Transmit: For the heavier FTP lifting.
    • MarsEdit: For writing, publishing, and editing this site.
    • Adium: For getting to know my fellow webizens more personally.
    • CoverSutra: Quick, responsive control of iTunes. And Last.fm sync.
    • Aperture: Photo management. Preferred over Lightroom.
    • Notational Velocity: A handy desktop editor for my Simplenote account.
    • Caffeine: For keeping my MacBook awake when I’m otherwise engaged.
    • CloudApp: For sharing most anything.

  3. Don’t install what I don’t need. This includes:
    • Extra language support in OS X: All I speak is English.
    • The entire iLife suite: I’ve never used any of it, except iPhoto, which has been replaced by Aperture.
    • Microsoft Office for Mac: I’m going to do my best to get by with just iWork. Even when dealing with documents from my corporate work environment or PC-centric graduate school.
    • No Adobe anything: No Flash, no Photoshop, no nothing. I never use any of it, anyway.
    • No extra browsers: Not Chrome. Not Firefox. Not RockMelt (Does anyone actually use that?). Safari is more than adequate.
    • No extra Twitter clients: I’m keeping Tweetie because, as I said, it’s home. But I certainly don’t need a half-dozen others hogging up space for good measure.
    • No Skype, FaceTime, etc.: Running my MacBook in clamshell, as I normally do, leaves me without a video camera. No camera = no video chat. Deleted.
    • No trial versions: I’m going to do my best to avoid the temptation to “try” new software based solely on the buzz. I’ll need to be thoroughly convinced that I’ll really use something, and be prepared to commit to doing so, before I’ll download it.

Of course, it must be said that there’s no inherent value in doing things how I do them. This is just what works for me. It suits my needs. If I suddenly found tomorrow that I needed to use 60GB to get things done properly, I’d do so. You should do the same.

As I mentioned above, I didn’t get to this point out of some coordinated effort to use less. Less is not more. Enough is enough. And for me, this is enough.

I’ll leave you with a quote from the Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius, which I am shamelessly quoting from The Focus Manifesto:

Most of what we say and do is not essential. If you can eliminate it, you’ll have more time, and more tranquillity. Ask yourself at every moment, “Is this necessary?”

If it’s not necessary, let it go. You’ll be happier.
