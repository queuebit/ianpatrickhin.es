DISQUS

It goes without saying that there has been a long-standing debate in the blogging community about whether to have comments. In the opposition corner you have folks like John Gruber, who once said that he doesn’t have comments because he wants to own every pixel on his site, from the top-left to the bottom-right. On the other side you have the likes of Sam Brown who loves having comments enough to personally reply, via email, to each and every one he receives.

I’ve gone back and forth on the issue. The last time I had comments enabled was nearly a year ago. While I really enjoyed them, I never re-enabled them once I brought my site back from hiatus this past August. This was mostly due to laziness, but also partly to do with Tumblr’s built-in ability to “Like” and “Reblog” a post. Frankly comments on Tumblr seemed redundant at best. If people liked your post, they could say so easily enough.

But this is less true on WordPress. Blogging on WordPress is a far more solitary task than on Tumblr, and I have definitely found that—if nothing else—I miss the interactivity Tumblr provided. Which brings me to DISQUS.

DISQUS is an independent service that integrates with WordPress to provide a truly robust community feedback platform. It supports Tumblr-style “Liking” of posts, threaded comments, verified login, and sharing via Twitter and Facebook. It also aggregates “Reactions” to posts from across the web, so you can follow the conversation even if it happens off-site (as it so often does). It’s pretty powerful, and if you can’t tell I’m pretty excited about.

On my Colophon page I describe this site as:

my home on the web—a soapbox from which I can start, or continue, conversations with the many great people I’ve met online.

A conversation (normally) requires more than one voice. So please, join the discussion. “Like” things you enjoy. “Dislike” (yes, you can do that) things you don’t. Chime in. And, if you feel so inclined, push those comments out to Twitter. I think that it will make the experience more enjoyable for all of us.
