Tomorrow, the Pope joins Twitter (@pontifex).

I know what many of you must be thinking: “So what? Another guy joins Twitter. There are lots of guys on Twitter.” And it’s true — there are lots of people on Twitter. But this is different.

Even if you’re not Catholic — and of course I am — you should recognize the cultural and historical importance of the papcy. It is one of the most enduring institutions in human history, beginning with Saint Peter (himself an apostle of Jesus Christ). And though it is well known for it’s traditionalism, it has been flexible enough to adjust to more than 2,000 years of changing times.

Tomorrow the 85-year old pontiff will ease himself into social media for the first time. But don’t expect him to say anything infallible; in fact, the entire notion that popes are regularly redefining truth and issuing bold moral dictates is false. In fact, in the 142 years since the First Vatican Council proclaimed the dogma of papal infallibility, the pope has only defined a dogma one time (the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary). Instead, you should expect him to speak as he always does: on Christian faith, ecumenism, charity and the defense of human rights and dignity.

From a purely objective perspective, the pope’s first tweet is an historic event: the embodiment of one of the world’s oldest and most enduring institutions is openly embracing one of its newest and most fleeting mediums for communication. Whatever he says — and obviously it’ll only be 140 characters — will be a tweet for the history books, as was the first papal radio address.

If you’re a Catholic, a history buff or just a fan of the internet, tomorrow is a day to remember.
