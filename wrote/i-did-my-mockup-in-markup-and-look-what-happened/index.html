I Did My Mockup in Markup, and Look What Happened

A few weeks ago I shared an article on 24ways.org—“Make Your Mockup in Markup” by Meagan Fisher—that argued “a website’s design should begin where it’s going to live: in the browser.” At the time I mentioned that, as a hobbyist web-designer with little-to-no graphic design experience this walkthrough was incredibly helpful. At the time I shared the link I hadn’t yet attempted to put Meagan’s advice to practice; yesterday I did, and look what happened.

The current design of this website is a direct result of implementing the “Mockup in Markup” approach, and I have to say that I’m quite pleased. Essentially it’s nothing more than a very content-centric design (with absolutely no graphics aside from my photograph on the about page) coated in a layer of Helvetica framed in a 960px Grid. But, perhaps not surprisingly, that’s all it took. Of course, that was a no brainer decision for me: I’ve never been big on graphic and imagery in websites, particularly in blogs. I prefer to keep it simple and content focused, which hopefully this design does.

One thing Meagan emphasized a lot—and which I’ve heard echoed among a lot of designers I admire—is the logic & value of putting content before design so that the ultimate design compliments and enhances the content. I can’t think of any environment in which this is more important—and, ideally, more simple—than on a blog in which I write all the content. I had a wealth of content to work with during the design process, and as I tested the various templates (text, links, quotes, etc.) I was able to test them with my content, rather than using Lorem Ipsum. I think that the result was a design that conveyed the tone of my voice & personality, as it should.

This was also my first attempt at hand-coding a Tumblr theme from scratch so I’m sure there are things I’ve overlooked & bugs to be worked out. One of the benefits of using your own design as a guinea pig is that you have to live with whatever bugs you’ve created, which forces you to fix them more quickly. Please be patient as I work through them over the next several weeks.

Lastly, because this was the first time I’ve build a Tumblr theme a few things werebrought to my attention that I had not previously considered: (1) If you’re using the Hi-Res Photo tag it should auto-default to the 500px image if no Hi-Res Photo is available so that people using wide layouts (like this one) can use the Hi-Res image as the default, and (2) You should be able to theme the Archives so that they appear to be part of a cohesive site. Otherwise theming Tumblr was a dream: very simple, very versatile.

All that said, I’d love to hear your thoughts! You can reach me by email or twitter almost anytime.
