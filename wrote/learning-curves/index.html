Learning Curves

After sharing Rob’s post about why you shouldn’t switch to the new cool software just because all the “cool kids” are doing it, I noticed that he’d posted a more recent piece about why he wasn’t interested in switching to OmniFocus himself. Chief among them was that the learning curve for OmniFocus was simply too steep to be worthwhile.

I disagree. Vehemently.

Of course, there’s no arguing that OmniFocus has a steep learning curve. In fact, the Mac version is amongst the most complex and feature-rich applications I’ve ever used. It is, at times, confusing. And, yes, it comes with a 60+ page user manual. But that’s part of what makes OmniFocus so fantastic.

Permit Me An Analogy

An automobile’s purpose it to get you from Point A to Point B faster than walking, and in many cases to bring cargo along, as well. Walking, of course, it simple—you’ve been doing it for nearly your whole life. If you just wanted to get going with the least amount of friction, walking would be the way to go without a doubt.

Driving a car, however, is anything but simple. There are buttons, gauges, and -ometers galore. There’s a wheel, and pedals, and mirrors. And then, to top it all off, there are all these rules you have to learn before you’re even allowed to use it on the road. All this takes time, effort, and commitment. But the payoff is well worth it, because once you’ve mastered the complex system of the automobile you’ll be able to traverse vast distances far, far more quickly and efficiently than you ever could have on foot.

My Analogy, Explained

Most people start out managing their to-do lists as, well, to-do lists—just like most people start getting around primarily by walking. It’s easy, it’s self-explanatory, and there’s very little friction.

But many people will, eventually, find that their needs have changed and that the simplicity of to-do lists just aren’t scaling to their current needs. For those people, there’s the GTD framework.

But, like driving, GTD (and, therefore, OmniFocus) has a steep learning curve. To implement it fully (and you really can’t do it any other way) you’ll need to change the way you approach your stress, learn a new system of organizing your thoughts and responsibilities, and likely re-work your entire work-flow. This, again, takes a lot of time, effort, and commitment. It’s not at all frictionless—at least not in the beginning—but the long-term benefits are well worth the investment.

We Don’t Want Software For Dummies

I’m just as big of a fan of software that does one thing well as anyone, but I think we’re missing the mark if we start dismissing software as simply because it’s complicated, or has a steep learning curve. That OmniFocus has tutorial videos and a long user manual do not make it a bad piece of software. Quite the contrary. OmniFocus was built to implement a complicated and thoughtful system, and so it is, by necessity, a complicated and thoughtful application.

Shawn Blanc used a great simile in his OmniFocus review: OmniFocus is like a sledgehammer. It’s not too big, or too small—neither too simple, nor too complex—it’s perfectly suited to the task for which it was intended.

Like Cultured Code recently said, sometimes “to stop worrying about [something requires] worrying about it a lot at first.” The folks at OmniGroup set out to make the best GTD software available, and they succeeded. But like an automobile, it requires some training to use properly.

And that’s okay.
