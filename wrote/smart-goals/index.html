SMART Goals

When I graduated college, I embarked on a career as a public school teacher in my home city of Baltimore. Full of enthusiasm, but having no background in education, I matriculated into the Baltimore City Teaching Residency program.

BCTR, which is essentially a subsidiary of The New Teacher Project, recruits, trains, and supports non-traditional teacher candidates in order to provide inner-city schools with effective teachers. Though I ultimately left the teaching profession, the experiences I had during my time in the program changed my life forever. The most significant of these was my introduction to the idea of SMART Goals.

What Are SMART Goals?

According to Wikipedia, SMART is a mnemonic used to describe a set of criteria by which an individual or group can assess the goals or objectives for a project. That’s certainly accurate. But to me, SMART is much more like GTD, i.e., an idea that is, quite simply, life changing.

The entire concept grows out of the idea that the entire purpose for having a goal is to achieve it. Seems simple enough. But all too often our goals are ambiguous, or as David Allen would say, “not actionable.” We all have these sorts of “squishy” goals in our lives (ex: “prepare the house to go on the market”). They sound nice, but what does success look like? Certainly different than the status quo, but aside from that it’s really hard to say.

The idea behind SMART goals is to rethink these “squishy” goals to make them into something achievable, measurable, and valuable. As I mentioned, SMART is a mneumonic, or learning tool, designed to help you remember the following attributes that should be applicable to each and every goal you set:

  • Specific: Goals should be articulated as narrowly as possible, to facilitate easier visualization of the desired outcome.
  • Measurable: Each goal must contain certain criteria by which success or failure can be measured concretely.
  • Actionable: Focus on things that you can do. Now. With the skills and resources you have at your disposal. Doing anything else will simply result in frustration and resignation.
  • Realistic: If your goals are too ambitious, your mind will subconsciously concede defeat before you even begin. Set your sights high, but keep your target within reach.
  • Time-Bound: Set a deadline for completion. Without a hard deadline, procrastination will become your undoing.

Why Use SMART Goals?

These seem like common sense principles, but my experience has shown me that they are rarely implemented in either the personal or professional world. If they were, a great number of our seemingly “intractable” problems would be resolved rather a great deal more swiftly than we’d imagined possible.

To refer to a previous example, a squishy goal like “prepare the house to go on the market” is so broad that is is practically un-achievable. How will you know whether the house is ready? Without specific criteria, you’ll never know for certain and will therefore be continually stressed. That’s what makes SMART goals so great: unlike squishy goals they’re designed to be completed, not fretted over.

Turning Squishy Goals into SMART Goals

To turn a squishy goal into a SMART goal requires three basic steps: (1) visualize success, (2) define what it looks like, and (3) set a deadline.

For example, a typical squishy goal might sound something like this:

Get the house ready to go on the market.

Sounds like something you’ve had on a to-do list recently? I thought so. This is the sort of goal that comes up in conversation—say, in the car on the way to the store. It’s a start, in that it describes what you want, but it’s ultimately junk because it doesn’t tell you anything about what being “ready to go on the market” looks like.

However, visualize success (i.e., house is ready to go on the market), define what it looks like (i.e., a fresh coat of paint has been added and clutter has been removed), and set a deadline (say, the first day of Spring) and you’ve got yourself a SMART goal:

By the first day of Spring we will have decluttered and painted the house a bright, neutral color so that we can put the house on the market.

Unlike the original goal, this SMART goal is actionable. Decluttering and painting are things that you can do. Moreover, they are things that you can meaure by your deadline: “Have they been done, or not? Lets check.”

In Short

Using SMART goals will allow you to convert those seemingly intractable goals you’ve been “meaning to get to” into concrete, actionable projects you can handle. Even if you’re not a GTD devotee, this simple shift in approach will increase your productivity (and the quality of your results) dramatically.

What’s more, SMART goals aren’t complicated at all. In fact they’re quite formulaic:

By (date / deadline) we will (measurable goal / definition of success) so that (desired outcome).

Again, all it takes is three basic steps: (1) visualize success, (2) define what it looks like, and (3) set a deadline.
