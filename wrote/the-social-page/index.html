The Social Page

For my part, I’ve owned my name as a domain (first ianhines.com, and now ianpatrickhines.com) for years — but I’ve usually used it as the home of my blog. I love my blog, but it’s not always the best place for a first impression; arriving at the blog’s homepage is like jumping into a conversation mid-stream, rather than making an introduction.

Back in January 2011,  Naz Hamid  tried to address this challenge with  The Personal Page, which he describes as:

[a] simple one-page website is a way for people to have a very quick and easy personable website that aggregates your activity and positions a simple logo, a portrait and some description text in a nicely-formatted manner.

I’ve been using the Personal Page for some time ( whois.ianhin.es), and it’s a great “about page” — perfect for making a first impression online. But I’ve always thought it fell a bit short in terms of connecting my social network graph.

I’ve long wanted a site in the vein of  Tom Watson’s, which acts as a sort of live-stream of his actions around the web. But his solution is an awfully complicated, Django based web-app — something generally out of reach for the average user and definitely difficult to maintain — so I’ve never gone too far down that road.

Then I saw  David Kaneda’s  homepage and thought: “this is perfect.”

It’s a simple, static one-page website that points visitors towards his various profiles, blogs and contact information. And it’s gorgeous. So I decided to that, with David’s site as inspiration, I would make my own. You can find it at  ianpatrickhines.com.

I’m calling it The Social Page. It’s built on the  Skeleton Framework, which means it’s fully responsive and mobile friendly. It’s also super-small in terms of file size, with the index.html file weighing in at all of 5 kb. And in the spirit of Naz’s “The Personal Page,” it’s available as a free, open source, forkable download on GitHub.

GitHub: The Social Page

If you decide to make use of it, I’d love to see the result. Drop me a note via  email  or  Twitter.

Note: the icons I’ve used are from  Symbolset, and are not open source. They are, however, only  $1 to download. So please be sure you do so — because you’re not a thief.
